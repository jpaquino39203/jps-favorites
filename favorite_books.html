A Tale for the Time Being by Ruth Ozeki (2013, Viking Press)

Emotional Payoff:

A Tale for the Time Being holds a special place in my heart. At the recommendation of a friend, I picked this book up in the summer of 2019 and ended up rediscovering my love for reading beyond school. With a compelling dual narrative that spans time and place, the novel cleverly integrates concepts of metaphysics with themes of depression, suicide, and the burden of societal and familial expectations. A story that ultimately asks what it means to be strong in the face of adversity, A Tale for the Time Being by Ruth Ozeki is one of the most inspiring novels I have ever read.

A Visit from the Goon Squad by Jennifer Egan (2010, Knopf)

Plot/Storytelling:

Emotional Payoff:

Putting A Visit from the Goon Squad on this list feels slightly wrong–if only because the “novel” is technically a series of thirteen loosely connected short stories, each told by a different narrator. This is a novel with a rich cast of compelling characters and a number of amazing stories to tell. Coincidentally, in Goon Squad, author Jennifer Egan almost never uses the same narrating format/POV twice; the novel bounces from third-person past tense to second-person present tense to even a story told entirely through PowerPoint slides. Some chapters take place in America, others in Africa or Italy. It truly is all over the place, but in an inventive and moving way that highlights the interconnectedness of people and their stories.

The Song of Achilles sets the bar for what fantasy romance novels can be. A retelling of the Greek myth of Patroclus and Achilles, author Madeline Kline takes a classic story of war and tragedy and turns it into a character-driven drama that explores the consequences of love and pride in a time of relentless war. Her characters feel so distinct, so alive. Her sense of time and world-building make it easy for you to lose yourself in the narrative. Knowing how the story ends before you even pick it up doesn’t soften the heartbreak when it finally arrives.

Plot/Storytelling:

Emotional Payoff:

What I love most about Everything I Never Told You is the way author Celeste Ng portrays the complicated nature of multiracial family dynamics, how generational trauma and the crushing weight of societal expectations can obstruct meaningful connection. Ng takes the time to develop each of her main characters and their unique struggles, demonstrating just how difficult it can be for families to communicate with one another, especially during times when they need each other most. I also have to give props to the novel’s accessible prose, great pacing, and thriller-like structure–all essential qualities of a page-turner. All-in-all, a wonderfully moving and terribly addictive family drama novel.

More Claire Keegan (this time in the form of a novella)! Small Things Like These centers on Bill Furlong, a middle-aged family man faced with a moral dilemma in 1980s Ireland. The story starts off at a slow, careful pace, introducing the world and the steady life Bill has built for himself and his family. When Bill stumbles across a situation that he is unfamiliar with but nonetheless knows to be wrong, he grapples with the choice now laid out in front of him: the choice to do something about it. The story is beautifully presented, simple in premise but potent in execution. Keegan’s precise and unflinching prose is on full display; she knows exactly what she wants to say and sets out to say it. This notion is reflected in the (perfect) ending, as the story finishes on a note of quiet resolve and self-possession, a moment of human grace in the face of uncertainty.
