"The Proxy Marriage" by Maile Meloy (2012, The New Yorker)

"The Proxy Marriage" is my go-to feel-good story. Having read both of Maile Meloy’s short story collections—Half in Love and Both Ways Is the Only Way I Want It—I’ve found that her stories consistently leave me with a quiet sense of awe. Her prose is concise and accessible, yet it carries real emotional weight. It feels effortless, but there's real craft behind it: she's able to articulate a wide range of emotions with carefully placed details or metaphors that give her stories so much color. In "The Proxy Marriage," she balances loaded themes like 9/11 and the consequences of the Iraq war with more relatable ones like unrequited love, chasing a dream, growing up. The narrative feels lived in and spans years without ever dragging or rushing through key moments, and concludes with a (mostly) happy ending that feels earned and leaves you with a warm, fuzzy feeling that lasts throughout the day.

"Neighbors" is probably the most fun I’ve ever had reading a short story. There's just something about the way Zach Williams turns a simple reconciliation-after-an-affair plot into something both inexplicable and exhilarating. "Neighbors" is a story that thrives on its own tension and leans into its growing sense of strangeness to drive the momentum; in it, we follow a narrator who struggles to make sense of an increasingly unfamiliar situation (My favorite line in the whole story: "He had to go. This time, I thought, he would, of course, go down. When he didn't, I saw how very small my understanding of or purchase over all of this was."). Right when you think he's about to have an epiphany of some kind, the moment slips away from him, seemingly forever. Then the story ends, and you’re left just as bewildered as the protagonist, yet the story feels whole (and lingers in your mind for days).

“When Stars Collide” is the shortest entry on this list–1550 words!–but that isn’t why it’s one of my favorite stories to revisit. This was my first real introduction to Ottessa Moshfegh, now one of my favorite writers. What really drew me into this story–a witty reimagining of the how-I-met-my-partner narrative–was Moshfegh's sense of irony. The plot centers on a struggling actor who is intentionally difficult to sympathize with, yet Moshfegh portrays him as both engaging and, at times, unexpectedly relatable. This, paired with Moshfegh's stark, economical sense of setting, make for a memorable character sketch that is darkly humorous and a touch melancholic. The last line–"That was Elizabeth: she was my emptiness."–is an especially memorable one, mostly because of the confused, sickly feeling it left me with the first time I read it.

I first encountered Ted Chiang’s “Anxiety is the Dizziness of Freedom” during my third year of undergrad, as part of an assigned reading for a class on science fiction. The story is set in a world much like ours, the most notable difference being that its people have developed the technology to communicate across parallel universes. Chiang uses that technology–incorporating concepts from the Many-Worlds Interpretation–to shine light on his characters, many of whom wrestle with guilt from past choices and the desire to make better ones. The narrative behind “Anxiety” is a human one, summed up perfectly by my favorite in-story quote: “‘None of us are saints, but we can all try to be better. Each time you do something generous, you’re shaping yourself into someone who’s more likely to be generous next time, and that matters.’” Though it remains grounded and pragmatic in its approach to redemption, it is also, at its core, a painfully hopeful narrative.

I've deliberately avoided the topic of criticisms in these lists because these are my favorites after all, and I would much rather discuss the reasons why I like them so much–that said, I can’t think of a single thing that I would change about “So Late in the Day.” This story is incredible. Claire Keegan demonstrates masterful command over every aspect of her narrative. Her prose is precise, economical, cutting. She deftly fosters a sense of dread through carefully placed details (a loss of appetite, a comment on the unnecessarily large number of pillows sitting on the couch) and disciplined pacing, taking her time in setting up the “pieces.” That feeling of dread doesn’t let up until the very last sentence–by which point you’re left reeling. It’s such an uncomfortable story to read, unflinching in its themes and delivery, but it also warrants–and rewards–multiple rereads. A powerhouse of a short story.
