Despite being one of my favorite movies, The Farewell is not something I rewatch often. Certainly it’s an amazing film, well-made and thought-provoking, with layered, sympathetic characters played by great, level-headed actors. It’s also terribly dispiriting. Director Lulu Wang’s offbeat brand of filmmaking adds layers of humor and humanity to an otherwise quietly morbid family drama. The film delves into the clash between Eastern and Western cultural norms, specifically regarding the concept of truth and how it is communicated in the context of a terminal illness. This movie is by no means an easy watch and will likely leave you with more questions than answers–which, in my view, adds to its strength. Any film that leaves its audience stunned and contemplative is doing something right.

Your Name. (Japanese: 君の名は。) is a wonderful time from start to finish. As the only animated entry on this list, visually speaking, this movie is gorgeous. Every backdrop is beautifully drawn and meticulously detailed. Characters’ movements are expressive and fluid. The “camera work” is dynamic. Add in a soaring musical score, solid voice performances, and an engrossing fantasy plot with a shocking and well-executed twist–and you have a timeless animated classic (as well as my go-to comfort movie).

Return of the Jedi has one undeniable advantage over the other movies on this list: nostalgia. Growing up, I watched this movie on DVD all the time. It was–and still is–my favorite of the Star Wars films, for its amazing space battle finale, its iconic dialogue and performances, its themes of rebellion and resisting despair. In addition to being enchantingly entertaining, this is a movie that informed my values during my childhood, and every rewatch only confirms that those values still resonate just as deeply today. The Star Wars saga is one of hope and redemption, something Return of the Jedi embodies perfectly.

What sets Spider-Man 2 apart from a lot of other comic book movies is the amount of characterization given to its protagonist, Peter Parker/Spider-Man. Peter, like many of us, struggles to find balance in his life; his grades are falling, he’s always showing up late for work, he’s behind on his rent. The people in his life think him unreliable–all because of his crime-fighting responsibilities. Simultaneously, the villain Otto Octavius–brought to life by the mesmerizing Alfred Molina–battles compelling demons of his own. These personal conflicts are the driving force of this movie, and they give every action sequence–including the iconic “train fight” sequence–so much more meaning. Of course, none of these elements would be nearly as effective without director Sam Raimi’s unique style (a whole lot of whip pans), which renders the movie a near-perfect blend of drama, humor, and action.

The term “quietly devastating” comes to mind when I think of Aftersun, which centers on Calum and Sophie, a father and daughter vacationing together at a beach resort; decades later, an adult Sophie reflects on this time spent with her father in an effort to understand him. This is a semi-autobiographical film from writer and director Charlotte Wells, who does an excellent job of blending different narratives–young Sophie, adult Sophie, and Calum–each with their own themes and sensitivities. Leads Paul Mescal and Frankie Corio–who play Calum and young Sophie, respectively–play their parts so naturally. Their rapport feels authentic, capturing both the awkward and tender parts of a parent-child relationship. The cinematography, which makes considerable use of long takes and dialogue pauses, only enhances the film’s nuanced and realistic tone. It’s a deeply personal film, effective in its emotional delivery but also distinct in its voice.
